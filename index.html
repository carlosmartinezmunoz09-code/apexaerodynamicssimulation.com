Cf (skin friction coefficient) = τwall / (½ρU²) — the dimensionless ratio of wall shear stress to freestream dynamic pressure. It measures how hard the airflow is dragging on the surface.

Cf is highest near the leading edge where the boundary layer is thinnest (steep velocity gradient → high wall shear). It decreases chord-wise as the boundary layer thickens. Above M ≈ 0.72 (critical Mach) the accelerated upper-surface flow goes locally supersonic, forming a shock that moves forward with increasing Mach — from ~65% chord at M 0.72 to ~30% chord near M 1.0. The shock produces a sharp Cf spike. The rear recirculation bubble (adverse pressure gradient zone) is coupled to the shock: the post-shock thickened boundary layer carries less momentum into the APG, so the bubble shifts aft and grows broader as the shock moves forward.
